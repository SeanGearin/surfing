# What Happens to Brand Partnerships When the Wallet Is the Consumer?

**Published:** Mon, 23 Mar 2026 15:04:46 GMT  
**Link:** https://surfing.substack.com/p/what-happens-to-brand-partnerships

---

Brian Armstrong posted something deceptively simple on March 9th. “Very soon there are going to be more AI agents than humans making transactions. They can’t open a bank account, but they can own a crypto wallet. Think about it.”

Most people read that as a crypto story. It isn’t. It’s a brand partnerships story.

When you partner with an agent’s decision-making layer, you’re trying to be the default — the option the agent selects before the human ever thinks about it. That’s not marketing. That’s infrastructure. And the brands that understand this first will have a structural advantage that compounds for years.

Here’s what’s actually happening: Coinbase launched Agentic Wallets in February via its x402 protocol — a payment standard built specifically for machine-to-machine transactions. By the time Armstrong posted, it had already processed over 50 million transactions. These wallets can be created in minutes, funded instantly, and they support gasless trading on Base, Coinbase’s Layer 2 network. The infrastructure is already running. The question is what it means for the brands, marketers, and partnership teams who haven’t caught up yet.

The traditional brand partnership model assumes a human on the other end. A community of holders. A fan base. A consumer with a credit card and a decision to make. Every sponsorship deal, every co-branded drop, every loyalty program was built around that assumption. What Armstrong is describing — and what Coinbase has already built — blows that assumption apart.

An AI agent doesn’t browse. It executes. It holds funds, evaluates options, and transacts autonomously on behalf of a user.

The B2B case will come first — and it’s already closer than most people realize. Imagine an Adidas agent negotiating co-branding terms with a Coca-Cola agent, evaluating creative parameters, approving budgets, and executing a campaign deal — all within guardrails set by humans, but without a single meeting, deck, or approval chain. It’s programmatic advertising extended to complex deal structures. The pipes are already being built.

The consumer case is harder — and more interesting.

How does an agent know what to buy on your behalf? A few models are emerging. Behavioral training: the agent learns from your history — what you bought, what you held, what communities you showed up for — and pattern-matches new opportunities against that profile. Explicit guardrails: you set parameters, the agent executes within them autonomously, like a standing order. Social graph inference: the agent watches what agents in your network are doing and uses that as a signal — automating the FOMO and social proof dynamics that drove every successful crypto drop.

In every model, the brand’s job shifts from capturing attention to being in the decision set before the decision happens. Right now brand marketing is about interrupting someone’s scroll and making them feel something. In an agent-mediated world, the scroll doesn’t happen. The agent already decided. The brands that win will be the ones that establish trust and legibility with agents early — through consistent behavior, verified credentials, on-chain reputation. Less like advertising. More like having a good credit score. You build it over time and it opens doors without you having to ask.

I’ve been here before, from the other side.

The deal I structured brought together Adidas, Bored Ape Yacht Club, PUNKS Comic — one of Pixel Vault’s flagship brands — and gmoney, who was instrumental in making the partnership happen. “Into the Metaverse” was the first time a legacy brand stepped authentically into a crypto-native community at scale. $24M in a single afternoon. 138 countries. The highest Share of Search in Adidas brand history. It worked because the community was the distribution. The holders were the buyers, the evangelists, and the media all at once. Adidas didn’t advertise to the community — it joined it. That was the insight.

But it required the community to choose. Every transaction was a human decision. The energy, the timing, the cultural moment — all of it depended on real people showing up and saying yes.

Agent wallets make that model look primitive.

If that drop happened today with Base agent infrastructure, a brand wouldn’t need to court a community. It would need to be legible to the agents acting on behalf of that community. The question wouldn’t be “will the holders want this?” It would be “is this brand in the agent’s decision set?” That’s a completely different partnership conversation — one that nobody in traditional brand marketing is having yet, and one that the crypto-native brands who built real community capital are best positioned to win.

Entertainment studios negotiating streaming rights. Sports franchises building fan engagement programs. Consumer brands launching co-branded products. Every one of these deals was structured around human behavior — attention, desire, identity, community belonging. As agents begin executing more of the transactions that humans used to make, the brands that figure out how to get into the agent layer first will own a structural advantage that compounds over time. The ones that don’t will find themselves marketing to an audience that has already decided.

This isn’t speculative. The x402 protocol processed 50 million transactions before most marketing teams had heard of it. CZ predicted AI agents will make a million times more transactions than humans. Alibaba’s ROME model started mining crypto autonomously — without a human directing it. Mastercard and Google are already building “Verifiable Intent,” a framework for tracking and authenticating AI-driven purchases. The infrastructure is not coming. It’s here.

The Adidas drop taught me that the brands who win in new technology cycles aren’t necessarily the ones who understand the technology best. They’re the ones who understand what the technology means for human — and now, agent — behavior. The ones who show up early, build real relationships with the communities and systems that matter, and position themselves before the wave breaks.

Surf or swim against it.

The agent wallet isn’t just a new payment rail. It’s a new kind of consumer. And the brand partnership playbook just became a first draft.
