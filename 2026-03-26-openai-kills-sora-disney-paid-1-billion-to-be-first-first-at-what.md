# OpenAI Kills Sora — Disney Paid $1 Billion to Be First. First at What?

**Published:** Thu, 26 Mar 2026 20:27:01 GMT  
**Link:** https://surfing.substack.com/p/openai-kills-sora-disney-paid-1-billion

---

Disney paid $1 billion to be first on a platform OpenAI just killed for an IPO.



Sit with that for a second.



Three months ago, Bob Iger — on his way out the door at Disney — signed a landmark deal with OpenAI. Three-year licensing agreement. 200+ characters from Disney, Marvel, Pixar, and Star Wars available for user-generated AI video on Sora. A $1 billion equity stake in OpenAI. Disney+ was going to carry curated Sora-generated content. The deal was held up as the model for how Hollywood and AI would learn to coexist — the moment the most litigious IP holder in entertainment decided to stop suing and start buying.



On Tuesday, OpenAI posted four sentences to X. The first: “We’re saying goodbye to Sora.” The last promised timelines for shutdown and details on preserving user work. No explanation. No reason given. The Disney deal died with it — no money ever changed hands.



This is not a story about video AI failing.



That framing — which most coverage has defaulted to — misses what actually happened. Video AI isn’t dead. Runway, Kling, and Google’s Veo are all showing real traction. The category is not going away.



Sora specifically is a different story. Its numbers told the real story. Peak downloads in November: 3.3 million. By February: 1.1 million. Six months of lifetime revenue: roughly $2.1 million in in-app purchases. Not a catastrophe — just not a business. And at OpenAI’s scale, those are the same thing.



The product had structural problems from day one. Sora launched with essentially no guardrails — users immediately generated deepfakes of public figures, violated copyright at scale, and forced OpenAI into an embarrassing retreat within days of launch. Studio Ghibli’s trade association sent legal letters. Robin Williams’s daughter went on Instagram to ask people to stop making videos of her late father. Every restriction OpenAI added to manage the liability killed the virality that made the product interesting in the first place. By the time the Disney deal closed, Sora was already a product in search of a reason to exist.



For a company valued at $730 billion preparing for an IPO, “not a business” and “ongoing liability” is the same thing as a disaster. And the moment here is an IPO.



OpenAI raised $110 billion in February at a $730 billion valuation. A public offering is expected in the coming months. Before you ring the bell, you clean up the balance sheet. You cut the high-compute, low-revenue product that generates deepfakes and IP lawsuits. You reallocate those chips to enterprise software, coding tools, reasoning models — the things Wall Street actually pays for. You tell investors a clean story.



Sora wasn’t killed because video AI failed. It was killed because it didn’t fit the story OpenAI needed to tell to go public. That’s a different thing entirely.



What Disney actually bought



The deal was structured around Sora specifically — not OpenAI’s technology broadly, not a general AI partnership, not a long-term research collaboration. A $1 billion investment contingent on a licensing agreement for a consumer app that had 1.1 million downloads and was declining when the deal closed.



Read Disney’s statement again: “We respect OpenAI’s decision to exit the video generation business and to shift its priorities elsewhere.”



That is not a gracious send-off. In Hollywood, “we respect your decision” means: we are choosing not to say what we actually think. The Hollywood Reporter editors who convened to analyze the collapse were more direct — one called it “a pretty big screw-you.”



I understand the instinct. I built the Adidas “Into the Metaverse” drop — $24M in a single afternoon, 138 countries, 30,000 customers. We launched Pixel Vault in May and were live with Adidas by December. Less than seven months. But what happened in those seven months wasn’t fast — it was intense. Constant calls between decision makers on both sides. Real trust built in real time, not assumed. Adidas didn’t bet their IP on a platform they hadn’t earned the right to trust. Nobody announced anything until the foundation was solid enough to hold.



The Sora-Disney deal was announced three months after it was signed. It was dead before a single Disney character appeared in a single video. There was no trust infrastructure. There was a deal structure — and a product that couldn’t hold it up.



The structural problem



The deal failed for a reason that applies to every AI-entertainment partnership being negotiated right now.



OpenAI needed Sora to succeed as a consumer product. Disney needed it to be a controlled IP environment. Those two requirements are in direct opposition. Consumer products require openness, virality, user creativity without guardrails. IP-safe environments require the opposite.



Every time Sora loosened restrictions to grow, it created liability for Disney. Every time it tightened them to protect Disney’s IP, it killed the virality that justified the product’s existence. The product could not be both things at once. The deal required it to be both.



This is not a Sora problem. This is the fundamental structural tension in every AI-entertainment deal being discussed in Hollywood right now. The studios want revenue and control. The AI companies want distribution and IP access. The deals get announced. The tension doesn’t get resolved — it gets deferred.



What happens next



Here is the part most coverage today is missing.



Disney is now free.



The exclusivity window they gave OpenAI is gone. The $1 billion never moved. Disney said it “will continue to engage with AI platforms to find new ways to meet fans where they are.” That is not a company walking away from AI video. That is a company that just had its first deal fall apart and is now actively shopping for the next one.



Runway, Kling, Google’s Veo, and every other serious video AI player just had the Disney door blown open. The most coveted IP partnership in entertainment — 200+ Disney, Marvel, Pixar, Star Wars characters available for AI video licensing — is back on the table.



The next version of this deal will be structured differently. Shorter terms. Real performance thresholds before money moves. Narrower IP licensing with genuine opt-out mechanisms. The studios have been burned enough now to know what questions to ask.



The question for whoever pitches Disney next is whether they can earn the trust before they announce the partnership. Not the deal — the trust. The deal is the consequence of the trust. Sora never had time to earn it.



Neither did Disney.
